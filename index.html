Push and Pushback in Contesting AI

Demands for and Resistance to Accountability

Much research examines how AI actors "from above"—designers, deployers, and authorities—handle accountability. Far less is known about contestation: the dynamic, iterative process by which affected communities and lower-power actors challenge AI systems. Drawing on 43 real-world cases spanning health, education, policing, and employment, we map who contests AI, what gets targeted, how institutions respond, and what outcomes emerge. Contestation is rarely settled cleanly—it is an ongoing negotiation shaped by attribution clarity, coalition strength, enforcement capacity, and strategic framing.

Conceptual Framework

What Is Contestation?

Contestation is a relational, iterative accountability-seeking process in which lower-power actors challenge AI systems—and those from above push back. Unlike simple algorithmic resistance or abandonment, contestation involves ongoing negotiation between parties with asymmetric power, shaped by Bovens's relational theory of accountability.

Key Findings

What Shapes Contestation Outcomes?

Five interconnected factors determine whether contestation leads to meaningful change—or is absorbed by institutions. These factors interact in non-linear ways, and lack in one cannot be offset by surplus in another.

FACTOR 01

Attribution & Tangibility of Harm

When harms are well-documented and traceable to specific actors, contestation is far more effective. Diffuse harms distributed across many systems or actors are structurally harder to contest.

Case evidenceClear attribution in Arkansas RUGS and K.W. v. Armstrong enabled court-ordered remedies. Diffuse algorithmic bias with unclear causal chains often stalled without resolution.
FACTOR 02

Coalition Strength & Visibility

Multi-stakeholder coalitions amplify contestation by increasing media visibility and reputational costs of inaction. High-profile individuals dramatically accelerate institutional response timelines.

Case evidenceThe U.S. Take It Down Act resulted from sustained coalition pressure. The UK A-Level algorithm was reversed within days under mass student-media coalition pressure.
FACTOR 03

Institutional Cost–Benefit Calculations

Institutions weigh the reputational, legal, and operational costs of continued contestation against the costs of change. When feasible alternatives exist, pivots happen faster.

Case evidenceCharacter.AI restricted minor access after regulatory pressure made inaction more costly than concession. Stanford revised its vaccine algorithm when operational alternatives were available.
FACTOR 04

Enforcement Mechanisms & Jurisdiction

Strong regulatory structures with real enforcement capacity enable binding outcomes. Cross-jurisdictional complexity and under-resourced regulators constrain what contestation can achieve.

Case evidenceClearview AI faced stronger enforcement via GDPR in the EU than in the US. The Anthropic copyright case benefited from clear US copyright enforcement authority with viable legal remedies.
FACTOR 05

Strategic Framing

Framing contestation around procedural rights and due process—rather than technical specifics—tends to enable broader policy discussions and stronger legal standing.

Case evidenceHouston ISD teachers and K.W. v. Armstrong both succeeded by foregrounding due process rather than algorithmic details, securing outcomes that technical framings may have foreclosed.
KEY INSIGHT

Non-Linear, Iterative Dynamics

These factors interact in complex, non-linear ways. Blocking one contestation avenue often prompts reframing and pursuit of alternative channels—with timing playing a critical mediating role.

Case evidenceWhen Clearview AI invoked BIPA exemptions to block US challenges, advocates shifted to EU GDPR enforcement. When Google used NDAs to restrict Project Nimbus disclosure, workers escalated to public protest.
Evidence-Based Guidance

Lessons for Stakeholders

Our analysis yields concrete, evidence-grounded lessons for actors across the contestation ecosystem—based on what worked, what didn't, and why.

  • 1

    Build Evidence of Harm and Attribution

    Systematically document algorithmic harms and identify responsible actors early. Create shared accountability forums—reporting platforms, community documentation hubs—to build evidence bases that sustain contestation over time.

  • 2

    Frame Contestation Strategically

    Frame around procedural rights and due process, not technical specifications. Ensure discussions address broader social and ethical considerations that cannot be easily obscured or delegitimized through technical counter-arguments.

  • 3

    Treat Implementation as a Distinct Stage

    Secure lasting pressure and enforcement beyond initial victories. Anticipate delayed implementation and prolonged resistance—many institutional "concessions" are strategic delays, not genuine reforms.

  • 4

    Lower Barriers and Enable Collective Action

    Develop practical reporting tools and complaint templates. Strengthen procedural rights and standardize complaint mechanisms to reduce the burden on individual contesters who often lack organizational support.

  • 5

    Build and Sustain Shared Infrastructure

    Maintain shared harm repositories and reusable legal and technical tools. Preserve knowledge and communities of practice across cases so hard-won insights are not lost between disputes.

  • 1

    Support Contestation Infrastructure

    Invest in accessible auditing tools, complaint platforms, and coordination mechanisms that lower the barriers to meaningful contestation—especially for individuals without organizational support.

  • 2

    Establish Procedural Rights with Real Enforcement

    Create rights to explanation and redress backed by meaningful enforcement. Symbolic rights without enforcement capacity reproduce institutional advantages rather than genuine accountability.

  • 3

    Design Governance Aligned with How Contestation Works

    Governance interventions must match the iterative, dynamic reality of AI contestation—not assume accountability is a one-time event. Build in mechanisms for ongoing monitoring and adaptive response.

  • 4

    Address Cross-Jurisdictional Gaps

    Harmonize enforcement across jurisdictions to prevent regulatory arbitrage where institutions exploit weak enforcement contexts to evade accountability for harms they cause globally.

  • 1

    Study Contestation as a Dynamic Process

    Move beyond snapshot analyses of individual harms or outcomes. The relational, iterative dynamics between contesters and institutions are where accountability is actually negotiated—and where research attention is most needed.

  • 2

    Attend to Less-Visible Contestation

    High-profile cases over-represent in available data. Methodological investment in capturing internal, organizational, and under-reported contestation is needed to understand the full landscape of how AI is challenged.

  • 3

    Theorize Non-Linear and Interactive Effects

    The five dynamics we identify interact in complex, non-linear ways. Future work should develop richer causal models of how these factors combine and interact across different domains and institutional contexts.
